SNail-游刃指尖

為了同時解決使用者輸入不便與穿戴舒適的問題，以及探索更具劃時代性的產品。我們提出了SNail。該裝置為一個感測手指上物理變化的智慧型指甲，我們利用指甲上微小的形變，可以偵測出手指的運動，進而知道使用者的手寫輸入。因為SNail是穿戴在不容易察覺的指甲上，所以使用者不會有不舒適感的問題，這是一個很大的優勢。偵測手指手寫的目的，是我們可以在任意的平面上，畫出不同的圖案或符號，當作智慧型裝置的輸入替代方案，也可以偵測手指筆跡，用來解鎖其他裝置、簽名或是不需要筆的寫字等。有了SNail，我們可以不用擔心沒有筆，也可以更輕易且舒適地與機器互通有無。

Node.js, Machine Learning, PCB Layout, Strain Sensing

EyeWatch

EyeWatch創新技術:智慧手錶結合EyeWatch技術，即可以將使用者手背變成一個觸控界面，就能在手背上做出各種手勢，與智慧手錶互動。EyeWatch讓使用者使用熟悉的觸控手勢卻解決了與螢幕小的智慧手錶互動中最大的問題:手指遮掩的問題(Fat Finger Problem)，並提供了更多直覺與方便的智慧手錶互動的可能性。EyeWatch在錶帶位置，裝上紅外線發射模組與微型攝影機，利用影像處理演算法 追蹤手指反射紅線形成的亮點區域計算出食指在手掌的位置與軌跡並透過手勢辨識演算法辨識使用者各種手勢。最後透過WiFi或Bluetooth與智慧手錶連結互動。

Arduino, IR Camera, Computer Vision

Fighting Gulliver

Pretend play is a play where children assign roles and then act them out. A child may even imagine himself as a giant among his toys in his play. To rebuild such pretend play, immersive gaming experience is required throughout the gameplay. We introduce Fighting Gulliver, featuring an innovative game design that integrates cutting-edge technologies including virtual reality (VR), body motion input methods, mobile devices, and wearable devices. The player with VR headsets and body motion capturing devices plays as the giant, while others without the two devices join forces to defeat the giant. Players playing character roles other than the giant are not limited on a desktop device. Entering the game through different devices results in different character roles, skills, and user interfaces. As a result, Fighting Gulliver is a cross-platform multiplayer online role-playing game (MORPG) with novel gaming experience and unique game settings rarely found in previous works. Game balance design and a user study are also discussed in this work.

Unity3D, Oculus Rift, Realtime Gaming, Cross Platform

EyeWrist

EyeWrist 創新技術：在任何時候將手掌變成一個觸控界面。使用者只要配戴 EyeWrist 智慧手環，就能隨心所欲在手掌上做出各種手勢，享受操控一切的快感。比方說，在手掌畫出音符圖樣，就能啓動手機裡的音樂 app ，或者畫出照相機 叫出相機功能，甚至手寫搜尋想看的電影。若嫌預設指令不夠，也可以自行創造手勢，新增更多功能。EyeWrist 在手環位置裝上紅外線發射模組與微型攝影機， 利用影像處理演算法追蹤手指反射紅線形成的亮點區域計算出食指在手掌的位置與軌跡並透過手勢辨識演算法辨識使用者各種手勢。在2014 DOIT Taiwan Open Innovation Festival 中受邀展出，在整理上百位使用者與業界專家的建議後，搭配LinkitOne 開發版，解決攝影機耗電與隱私的問題、OLED即使顯示手勢與互動裝置、單手互動的互動設計等。

Publications

Nail+: Sensing Fingernail Deformation to Detect Finger Force Touch Interactions on Rigid Surfaces • MobileHCI '16

Min-Chieh Hsiu, Chiuan Wang, Da-Yuan Huang, Jhe-Wei Lin, Yi-ping Hung, De-Nian Yang, Mike Chen

  Arduino, Node.js, CadSoft Eagle, PCB Designing, Strain Sensors

Force sensing has been widely used for bringing the touch from binary to multiple states, creating new abilities on interactions. However, prior proposed force sensing techniques mainly focus on enabling force-applied gestures on certain devices. This paper presents Nail+, a technique using fingernail deformation to enable force touch sensing interactions on everyday rigid surfaces. Our prototype is a 3x3 0.2mm strain sensor array mounted on a fingernail. We conducted a 12-participant study to evaluate the feasibility of this sensing approach for detecting normal and force-applied tapping and swiping. Results showed that the average accuracy for sensing among 10 gestures is 84.67%. We finally implemented two example applications using our Nail+ prototype for controlling the interfaces of head-mounted display (HMD) devices and remote screens.

ForceBoard: Using Force as Input Technique on Size-Limited Soft Keyboard • MobileHCI '16 Demo

Min-Chieh Hsiu, Da-Yuan Huang, Chi An Chen, Yi-ping Hung, De-Nian Yang, Mike Chen

  iOS Development, Force Sensing

Various typing methods of qwerty-based keyboards on smartwatches have been proposed in recent years. However, since each key can only occupy limited input space and our fingers are too big, recent solutions are mainly two-step typing methods. The two-step process is distant from our physical keyboard experiences, and require users to frequently change the keyboard layouts. This paper proposes a single-step typing technique that allows users to acquire keys by a single touch. For the keyboard layout, ForceBoard combines two adjacent keys into one region for more precise selections. Typing a character only requires a single step: soft presses for tap-key, and hard presses for press-key. We conducted a study comparing the performance of ForceBoard with other two state-of-the-art two-step methods. Our results showed that, ForceBoard outperformed ZoomBoard significantly, and slight better than SplitBoard 10.36% in average. Furthermore, ForceBoard also received higher preferences on text speed and satisfaction.

Giggler: An Intuitive, Real-Time Integrated Wireless In-Ear Monitoring and Personal Mixing System using Mobile Devices • MM '15

Andries Valstar, Min-Chieh Hsiu, Te-Yen Wu, Mike Y. Chen

  iOS Development, Socket IO, Audio Broadcasting

For live music performances, current Wireless In-Ear Monitoring and Personal Mixing setups require a lot of equipment and wiring. This paper introduces Giggler, a system that makes a Wireless In-Ear Monitoring and Personal Mixing experience, easier to setup, easier to use, faster to control and more accessible for musicians than conventional systems by integrating all equipment into one mobile device per musician. The results of the two user studies that we conducted show that Giggler's User Interface performs up to more than twice as fast as a traditional mixer and indicate that Giggler outperforms a Physical mixer because visual features are added to streamline the channel identification process.

PalmGesture: Using Palms as Gesture Interfaces for Eyes-free Input • MobileHCI '15

Cheng-Yao Wang, Min-Chieh Hsiu, Po-Tsung Chiu, Chiao-Hui Chang, Liwei Chan, Bing-Yu Chen, Mike Y. Chen

  Arduino, IR Camera, Computer Vision, Machine Learning, Node.js

We explored eyes-free gesture interactions on palms, which enables users to interact with devices by drawing stroke gestures on palms without looking at palms. We conducted a 24-person user study to understand how users draw gestures on the palm with varying characteristics including regions, orientation and starting points. Based on the findings, we proposed two new interaction techniques for palm-based gesture interface. To explore and demonstrate the feasibility of the interaction, we implemented EyeWrist, a wrist-mounted prototype which detects gestures on palms by using an IR camera and laser-line projector.

PalmType: Using Palms as Keyboards for Smart Glasses • MobileHCI '15

Cheng-Yao Wang, Wei-Chen Chu, Po-Tsung Chiu, Min-Chieh Hsiu, Yih-Harn Chiang, Mike Y. Chen

  Arduino, Google Glasses, VICON System

We present PalmType, which uses palms as interactive keyboards for smart wearable displays, such as Google Glass. PalmType leverages users' innate ability to pinpoint specific areas of their palms and fingers without visual attention (i.e. proprioception), and provides visual feedback via the wearable displays. With wrist-worn sensors and wearable displays, PalmType enables typing without requiring users to hold any devices and does not require visual attention to their hands. We conducted design sessions with 6 participants to see how users map QWERTY layout to their hands based on their proprioception.
